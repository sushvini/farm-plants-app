Endangered & Endemic Plants of My Farm

Western Ghats • Conservation • Storytelling

Syzygium caryophyllatum (Wild Jamun)

We called it the purple playground. As kids, we’d climb up its branches and pluck berries that turned our fingers into artwork. These little fruits shift from green to pink to deep violet—a fruit that teaches patience. But it’s fading now, with poor seed regeneration and vanishing groves. Birds and squirrels still search its branches for food—but fewer trees mean fewer feasts.

Common names: Clove-scented plum, South Indian plum, Kannada: ಕುಂಟ ನೇರಳೆ (Kunta Nerale)

Region: Western Ghats, Sri Lanka

Threat: Habitat loss, low regeneration

Conservation status: Endangered (IUCN Red List)

Hopea parviflora (South Indian Mahogany)

This tree once held up bridges, boats, and stories. Known locally as Bovu mara, its ironwood was the silent backbone of engineering marvels. Its timber was used for railway sleepers. Today, it finds fewer forests to anchor its roots—a tree that once carried empires across landscapes.

Common names: Bovu mara, Irubogam, Irumbogum, Kongu

Region: Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu

Threat: Logging, fragmentation

Conservation status: Endangered (IUCN Red List)

Myristica malabarica (Malabar Nutmeg)

Nestled in swampy sanctuaries, this tree produces rampatri—the spice that sings in kitchens. But its healing goes deeper. It’s shown promise in protecting the liver against cancer (Nature India, 2010).

Common names: Malabar Nutmeg, Kanage (Kannada), Kattujathikka (Malayalam)

Region: Karnataka and Kerala

Threat: Swamp degradation, overharvesting

Conservation status: Vulnerable (IUCN Red List)

Pterocarpus santalinus (Red Sandalwood)

The color of legacy. This heartwood gleams crimson, used in perfumes, rituals, and woodcraft. It’s treasured—and tragically overharvested. Smuggling has made it the victim of its own beauty. In Ayurveda, it’s used to treat skin disorders, purify blood, and carve sacred idols.

Common names: Red Sanders Tree, Lalchandan (Hindi), Raktachandan (Sanskrit)

Region: India (AP, TN), Sri Lanka

Threat: Illegal logging, smuggling

Conservation status: Endangered (IUCN Red List)

Memecylon umbellatum

Glossy leaves, purple fireworks in bloom. This understated tree thrives quietly in the shade, nurturing understory plants and pollinators. Its flowers beckon rare forest bees—a gentle anchor for ecosystem diversity.

Common names: Anjani (Marathi), Kaya (Tamil), Kaannaav (Malayalam)

Region: Western Ghats

Threat: Narrow range, deforestation

Conservation status: Endangered (IUCN Red List)

Canarium strictum (Black Dammar Tree)

If incense had a soul, this tree would be its heart. Its resin smells like memory—smoky, sacred, unforgettable. Birds and bees worship this tree. Over-tapping wounds it, yet it still gives.

Common names: Black Dhup, Raal

Region: Western Ghats

Threat: Over-tapping, forest degradation

Conservation status: Vulnerable (IUCN Red List)

Aporosa cardiosperma

It grows naturally, without help—a quiet strength that keeps biodiversity humming. Its fruits feed winged wanderers, keeping the forest’s rhythm alive.

Common names: Salle mara (Kannada), Vetti (Malayalam)

Region: Western Ghats

Threat: Logging, fire, climate change

Conservation status: Vulnerable (IUCN Red List)

Mammea suriga (Surige Tree)

My aunt first whispered its name to me. Her stories painted a flower so fragrant it felt mystical. I finally saw its name in a forest nursery, bought it, and brought it home like a dream fulfilled. These divine blossoms are used in rituals, worn like jewels. Think of it as nature’s Chanel No. 5—sacred, sensual, unforgettable.

Common names: Surangi (Hindi), Surige (Kannada), Surangan (Konkani)

Region: Western Ghats

Threat: Habitat degradation

Conservation status: Endangered (IUCN Red List)
